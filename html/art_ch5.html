Abstract Expressionism: The Freedom of Spontaneous Creation

Abstract Expressionism is a post-World War II art movement that emphasizes spontaneous, automatic, or subconscious creation. Artists like Jackson Pollock and Mark Rothko used bold colors and dynamic forms to convey deep emotions and personal visions. This movement rejects representational art, focusing instead on the act of painting itself. Abstract Expressionism was crucial in shifting the art world's center from Europe to the United States. Its impact persists in contemporary art through its influence on experimental and conceptual practices.
Impressionism: Capturing Fleeting Moments

Impressionism is a late 19th-century art movement characterized by its focus on capturing the effects of light and atmosphere in a momentary scene. Artists like Claude Monet and Pierre-Auguste Renoir used loose brushwork and vibrant colors to depict everyday life and natural landscapes. Unlike traditional art that emphasized detail and realism, Impressionism prioritized the perception of light and color over precise representation. This approach marked a significant departure from classical techniques and influenced future art movements. The legacy of Impressionism continues to shape how we view and interpret visual experiences.
